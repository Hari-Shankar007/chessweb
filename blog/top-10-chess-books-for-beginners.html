Why Beginners Should Still Read Chess Books (Even in the Age of Apps)

Chess apps are wonderful. They are available at midnight, they gamify practice, and they never get impatient. But they have one limitation: they rarely explain the reason behind a move.

A good chess book is different. It talks to you. It walks you through a position slowly, tells you what White is thinking, what Black is worried about, and why a particular idea works. That kind of explanation is hard to get from a computer engine that simply shows you a green arrow.

Here is why reading chess books still matters for beginners:

  • Concepts stick better when explained. A book that explains why a rook belongs on an open file is more memorable than an app that just marks your move as "?"
  • Books build independent thinking. When you study a chapter on tactics, you learn a pattern. The next time you see a similar position in a real game, your brain recognises it — even without a hint button.
  • Books slow you down in a good way. Beginners often rush moves. Sitting with a book and a board builds the habit of stopping, looking, and thinking before moving.
  • Books complement coaching perfectly. At EDUCHESS Academy, coaches often use the same ideas from books in class, creating a reinforcing loop between reading and live practice.

None of this means you should close your app and only read. The best approach is to use both — and this article will show you exactly how.

How to Choose a Good Chess Book as a Beginner

Walk into any bookshop or search online and you will find hundreds of chess books. Many of them are excellent — but for the wrong level. A book written for club players or tournament competitors will leave a beginner confused and discouraged.

Here is a simple checklist for choosing the right chess book:

  • Check the target level. Look for phrases like "for beginners," "0–1400 rated players," or "no prior knowledge required." If the first two pages mention the Sicilian Defence without explaining it, the book is probably too advanced.
  • Look for clear explanations, not just positions. A good beginner book explains why, not just what. Diagrams paired with plain-language reasoning are far more valuable.
  • Prefer books with exercises or puzzles. Reading passively is useful. Solving puzzles actively is even better. The best beginner books mix explanation with practice positions.
  • Consider the format for kids vs. adults. Children (roughly age 5–12) learn better from larger diagrams, simple language, friendly illustrations and short chapters. Adults can handle denser text.
  • Start with one book — not ten. Pick one that fits your level, read it cover to cover, and only then move on.

Top 10 Chess Books for Beginners

The books below are listed in a rough order of difficulty — starting with the absolute simplest and moving towards early-intermediate level. All are suited to players rated roughly 0–1400.

01

Chess for Children — Murry Chandler & Helen Milligan

Best for Complete Young Beginners

Who it is for

Children aged roughly 5–10 who are learning chess for the very first time. Parents who want to teach their child the basics at home will also find this book extremely accessible.

What it teaches

The book introduces every piece — how each one moves, how it captures, and what it is worth — through short stories featuring a cat named Kassy. It covers all the rules (including castling and en passant), basic checkmate patterns, and simple tactics. The language is deliberately simple and the diagrams are large and clearly labelled.

How to use it: Read one chapter together with your child each evening. After finishing the chapter, set up the positions on a real board and play them out.
02

Chess Fundamentals — José Raúl Capablanca

Best Classic Foundation for Adults

Who it is for

Adult beginners and older teenagers who want to learn chess from one of the greatest players who ever lived. No previous knowledge beyond knowing how the pieces move is assumed.

What it teaches

Capablanca was world champion from 1921 to 1927, and this book — first published in 1921 — teaches the ideas that made him dominant: simple and correct moves, the importance of pawns, basic endgame technique, and the principle of placing pieces on active squares. Reading it feels like sitting across the board from a wise, patient teacher.

How to use it: Play through one annotated game or endgame example each evening on a real or digital board. Let each concept settle before moving on.
03

Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess — Bobby Fischer, Stuart Margulies & Don Mosenfelder

Best Puzzle Book for Beginners

Who it is for

Beginners of any age — children and adults alike — who want to start building a tactical eye. The book is structured so that you do not need to read it from start to finish; you work through puzzles at your own pace.

What it teaches

The book focuses entirely on checkmate patterns and tactics using a programmed learning format — you answer a puzzle, turn the page to check, and the difficulty slowly increases. Patterns covered include back-rank mates, discovered checks, and smothered mates.

How to use it: Do 15–20 puzzles a day. Keep a notebook and write down any pattern you find difficult — then come back to those pages a week later.
04

Winning Chess Tactics — Yasser Seirawan & Jeremy Silman

Best for Learning Tactical Patterns Clearly

Who it is for

Beginners who have learned the rules and played a few games but want to understand how combinations work. Particularly good for players aged 12 and above, and for adults.

What it teaches

Tactics are short sequences of moves — usually 2 to 5 moves — that win material or force checkmate. This book explains every major tactical theme: pins, forks, skewers, discovered attacks, and combinations involving multiple themes. Each chapter explains the pattern clearly and then gives practice positions.

How to use it: Study one tactical theme per week. Solve the exercises at the end of each chapter without looking at the answers first, even if you get some wrong.
05

Chess Tactics for Students — John Bain

Best Puzzle Workbook for Young Players

Who it is for

Children aged roughly 8–14 who are already playing regularly and want to sharpen their tactical eye. Widely used in school chess programmes and by junior coaches.

What it teaches

The book presents hundreds of tactical puzzles — all in the 1–2 move range — organised by theme: forks, pins, discovered checks, and more. Each puzzle is presented as a diagram with a simple question: "White to move and win." The repetition builds a deep pattern library.

How to use it: Treat it like a daily exercise book. Solve 10 puzzles in the morning before school or practice. Circle puzzles you get wrong and revisit them weekly.
06

Logical Chess: Move by Move — Irving Chernev

Best for Understanding Why Moves Are Played

Who it is for

Beginners and early-intermediate players (roughly 800–1400) who have been playing for a few months and want to understand the reasoning behind chess decisions. Excellent for adult learners.

What it teaches

Chernev explains every single move in 33 complete games, using plain English. Why did White develop the knight to f3 rather than e2? Why did Black delay castling? This book teaches opening principles, middlegame planning, and simple endgame technique — all through real games.

How to use it: Play through one game per week on a board. After each move, try to guess the next move before reading the explanation.
07

How to Win at Chess — Levy Rozman (GothamChess)

Best Modern Book for Online-First Learners

Who it is for

Beginners of all ages who are already comfortable watching chess on YouTube or playing online, and who want a modern, friendly introduction that matches how chess is played today.

What it teaches

Levy Rozman, known online as GothamChess, writes in the same enthusiastic voice he uses in his popular videos. The book covers the rules, basic tactics, opening principles, simple attack patterns, and how to think during a game — including how to avoid common beginner mistakes like moving the same piece twice in the opening.

How to use it: Read one chapter, then play five online games trying to apply exactly what you just read. This active connection between reading and playing speeds up improvement significantly.
08

Chess: 5334 Problems, Combinations and Games — László Polgár

Best for Building a Deep Puzzle Habit

Who it is for

Committed beginners and early-intermediate players who are serious about improvement and want a comprehensive puzzle resource they can use for months or even years. The legendary Polgár sisters trained with this book.

What it teaches

This massive book contains over 5,000 chess puzzles, starting from extremely simple one-move checkmates and gradually increasing in difficulty. The sheer volume builds a deep library of patterns in your mind. It is not a book you read — it is a book you work through over many months.

How to use it: Solve 10–15 puzzles per day in order. Do not skip ahead. The gradual difficulty increase is the method; trust it.
09

Silman's Complete Endgame Course — Jeremy Silman

Best Endgame Book for All Levels

Who it is for

Beginners all the way up to advanced players. The remarkable feature of this book is that it is organised by rating level — you read only the sections relevant to you.

What it teaches

Endgames are the final phase of the game, when most pieces have been traded and the kings become active. Many beginners neglect endgame study, which is a mistake. Basic knowledge of king-and-pawn endgames, rook endings, and the concept of the "opposition" can save or win many games that look drawn. Silman explains all of this in plain, sometimes humorous language.

How to use it: Read the beginner chapter first. Play through every example on a board. Only move to the next chapter once you can solve all the exercises without help.
10

The Steps Method (Workbooks 1 & 2) — Rob Brunia & Cor van Wijgerden

Best Structured System for Kids in a Chess Programme

Who it is for

Children learning chess in a structured programme — particularly students in junior chess clubs, school chess, or academies like EDUCHESS Academy. Coaches across Europe and India use this series widely.

What it teaches

The Steps Method is a series of workbooks (Step 1 and Step 2 are the beginner levels) that cover every fundamental skill in a logical sequence: basic piece movements, simple checkmates, elementary tactics (forks, pins, discovered check), opening principles, and basic pawn structures. Each workbook is full of exercises — the learning happens by doing.

How to use it: Work through exercises with a parent or coach. Complete one page per session, check answers together, and discuss any mistakes before moving on.

How to Study These Books Step by Step

Buying a book is the easy part. Actually studying it consistently is where most beginners struggle. Here is a simple, realistic plan:

  1. Choose one book, not five. Pick the book from the list above that best matches your level and learning style. If you are a child in a chess programme, ask your coach. If you are an adult beginner, start with Chess Fundamentals or Logical Chess: Move by Move.
  2. Set up a board when you read. This is non-negotiable. Playing through every position on an actual board — physical or digital — forces your brain to engage actively instead of passively scanning diagrams.
  3. Study in short, regular sessions. 30 minutes a day, four or five days a week, is far better than three hours once a week. Chess understanding builds through repetition and rest, not marathon cramming.
  4. Keep a chess notebook. Write down: the tactical patterns you learn, positions you find difficult, and questions for your coach. Reviewing your notes before your next coaching session makes the lesson much more productive.
  5. Apply what you read in real games. After studying a chapter on forks, play 5–10 games online looking specifically for forking opportunities. The gap between "understanding a concept" and "using it in a real game" closes only through practice.
  6. Review puzzles you got wrong. Return to difficult exercises once a week. Getting a puzzle wrong is not failure; it is the most useful feedback you can get.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should beginners read chess books or just use apps?

Both — but in different ways. Apps are excellent for daily puzzle practice and playing games, but they rarely explain the reason behind moves. A good chess book fills that gap by walking you through ideas in plain language. Think of apps as your daily exercise and books as your classroom: you need both to improve steadily.

Which chess book is best for absolute beginners?

For young children (aged 5–10), Chess for Children by Murry Chandler is the gentlest starting point. For adult absolute beginners, Chess Fundamentals by Capablanca or How to Win at Chess by Levy Rozman are both excellent choices. The right answer depends on your age and how you prefer to learn.

How many chess books should a beginner read?

One at a time. Beginners often buy several books and end up finishing none of them. Pick a single book that matches your level, complete it fully, then move to the next. Two or three well-studied books will do far more for your game than ten books skimmed and abandoned.

Are chess books still useful for kids in the age of YouTube and apps?

Absolutely. Chess books teach children to slow down and think systematically — a skill that screens often discourage. A well-chosen book builds patience, reading comprehension, and independent problem-solving alongside chess skill. Many top junior players credit the habit of book study as a key part of their early development.

At what rating should I move from beginner books to intermediate books?

Once you can solve one-move and two-move tactics consistently, understand basic opening principles (develop pieces, control the centre, castle early), and handle simple king-and-pawn endgames, you are ready to move to intermediate material. For most players, this corresponds roughly to an online rapid rating of 1000–1200. Your coach at EDUCHESS Academy can advise on exactly the right moment to make that step.

Do I need a physical chess board to use these books?

A physical board is ideal — moving the pieces yourself engages your spatial memory far more than just reading diagrams. However, a digital board (such as a free board on Lichess or Chess.com) works perfectly well. The key is to never study a chess book without a board of some kind in front of you.

Can I use chess books alongside my EDUCHESS coaching sessions?

Yes — and EDUCHESS Academy actively encourages this. Coaches recommend specific books based on your level and current training focus, assign chapters as homework, and discuss book examples in class. Combining live coaching with book study creates a much stronger foundation than either approach alone.

The Bottom Line

Chess improvement is a long and rewarding journey. There are no shortcuts — but there is a good road. Consistent coaching, regular practice, and thoughtful book study will take any beginner, child or adult, from confusion to confidence one game at a time.
