Daniel Naroditsky Memorial Rapid & Blitz 2026: Sindarov’s Double Crown – Simple Kids & Parents Guide

📅 08/07/2026 • EDUCHESS Team

Summary: The Daniel Naroditsky Memorial Rapid & Blitz 2026 took place at the Charlotte Marriott City Center, Charlotte, North Carolina, USA from July 3–5, 2026. It was the first ever Naroditsky Memorial, a three-day chess festival created to honor GM Daniel Naroditsky (1995–2025), one of the world's most loved commentators and teachers. The event combined open rapid and blitz tournaments with a fun Creator Invitational and a dramatic Blitz Final for the top masters. Rising star GM Javokhir Sindarov swept the event, winning both the rapid and blitz Masters titles with strong, confident play. The Creator Invitational was won by IM John Bartholomew with a perfect 9/9 score. The chess world remembered Naroditsky through exciting games, friendly battles, and a celebration of his love for chess.

Tournament Overview

What is the Daniel Naroditsky Memorial?

Imagine hundreds of chess players — grandmasters, streamers, and kids — gathering in one big hotel for three days of nonstop rapid and blitz chess. That is exactly what the Daniel Naroditsky Memorial Rapid & Blitz is — a fast-paced festival where everyone plays quick games, meets their heroes, and remembers a great teacher of the game.

The event is named after GM Daniel Naroditsky, who was famous for his clear explanations, friendly streams, and strong speed chess skills. The idea is simple: celebrate chess the way Naroditsky did — with energy, kindness, and great attacking games.

Key facts at a glance

  • Dates: July 3–5, 2026
  • Venue: Charlotte Marriott City Center, Charlotte, North Carolina, USA
  • Format: 3-day rapid and blitz chess festival with open sections, a Creator Invitational, and a Blitz Final
  • Sections: Rapid Masters & Challengers, Blitz Masters & Challengers, Creator Invitational, Blitz Final
  • Time controls: Rapid – 10 minutes + 5 seconds increment; Blitz – 3 minutes + 2 seconds increment
  • Total prize fund: $50,000 guaranteed
  • Rapid Masters winner: GM Javokhir Sindarov
  • Blitz Masters champion: GM Javokhir Sindarov
  • Creator Invitational winner: IM John Bartholomew (9/9)

Main events of the festival

The Naroditsky Memorial was built around four connected events:

  • Rapid Championship (Friday): 7-round Swiss, Masters and Challengers sections, time control 10+5.
  • Blitz Championship (Saturday): 11-round Swiss, Masters and Challengers sections, time control 3+2.
  • Creator Invitational (Sunday): 9-round blitz round-robin between 10 famous chess content creators.
  • Blitz Final (Sunday): 9-round blitz round-robin among the top 10 Masters from the Saturday blitz.

Who Played?

Masters and Challengers

The Rapid and Blitz Championships were open tournaments, with separate Masters and Challengers sections so that strong grandmasters and ambitious club players could each face suitable competition. This made the event friendly for kids and adults of all rating levels, from beginners to professionals.

The Masters sections featured grandmasters and international masters fighting for rating points, prize money, and the special honor of winning the first Naroditsky Memorial. The Challengers sections gave lower-rated players their own podiums and trophies while still being part of the same big festival.

Creator Invitational lineup

On Sunday, the spotlight shifted to the Creator Invitational, where 10 popular chess streamers and educators faced each other in a 9-round blitz round-robin. The lineup included familiar faces from YouTube and Twitch, many of whom kids already know from online videos.

According to the official list, invited creators included IM Levy Rozman (GothamChess), WFM Alexandra Botez, Andrea Botez, IM Eric Rosen, IM Sagar Shah, WGM Dina Belenkaya, GM Aman Hambleton, IM Julien Song, IM John Bartholomew, and IM Danny Rensch.

Format & Time Controls

Rapid Championship – Friday, July 3

The Rapid Championship used a 7-round Swiss format in both Masters and Challengers sections. Each player had 10 minutes for the whole game plus a 5-second increment from move one, which is fast but still gives enough time to think.

Games were rated by both US Chess and FIDE, and the Rapid Masters event was part of the FIDE Circuit and US Chess Grand Prix. This made each game important, as good results could improve a player's rating and standings in official circuits.

Blitz Championship – Saturday, July 4

The Blitz Championship was even faster, with 11 rounds of blitz in each section. The time control was 3 minutes + 2-second increment, the classic modern blitz pace.

The top 10 finishers in the Blitz Masters qualified for the special Blitz Final on Sunday, where they would fight in a round-robin for the overall blitz title. This two-step system made Saturday feel like a big qualifying day before the elite showdown.

Blitz Final – Sunday, July 5

The Blitz Final was a 9-round round-robin between the top 10 Blitz Masters. The time control stayed at 3+2, but the field was smaller and stronger, meaning every game was critical.

This final stage decided the official Blitz Masters champion of the Naroditsky Memorial. It was here that Sindarov completed his double crown by staying undefeated and scoring 7.5/9.

Creator Invitational – Sunday, July 5

The Creator Invitational was also a 9-round blitz round-robin, using 3+2, but focused on entertainment as much as competition. Commentators described it as “two blitz tournaments” on the final day: one for creators and one for the masters.

For many young fans, this was the most exciting part: seeing their favorite YouTubers and streamers play each other live, blundering and brilliancing in equal measure. IM John Bartholomew stole the show by winning all nine games to take first place with a perfect score.

Day-by-Day Progression

Day 1 – Rapid: Sindarov Takes the Lead

On Friday, the Rapid Championship set the tone for the whole festival. In the Masters section, GM Javokhir Sindarov and GM Aravindh Chithambaram both scored 6/7, tying for first place after a series of sharp games.

The regulations called for a playoff when first place was tied. Sindarov and Aravindh played a two-game blitz tiebreak at 3+2, and Sindarov won 2–0, showing excellent nerves and attack in fast time controls.

For kids watching, these games were a perfect example of how to stay calm under pressure: Sindarov kept his king safe, used active pieces, and did not rush even when the clock was ticking down.

Day 2 – Blitz: Qualifying for the Final

Saturday’s Blitz Championship was all about speed and stamina. With 11 rounds in a single day, players needed good openings, fast calculation, and strong nerves to survive time scrambles.

In the Masters section, Sindarov continued his excellent form to finish among the top 10 and secure a place in the Sunday Blitz Final. Many games featured wild attacks, sacrifices, and mutual blunders — the kind of drama that makes blitz so much fun to watch.

These blitz games showed how important it is to know simple attacking patterns and basic endgames — even in very fast time controls.

Day 3 – Sunday: Creators & Blitz Final

Sunday was split into two main attractions: the Creator Invitational and the Blitz Final. In the Creator event, fans saw Levy Rozman, the Botez sisters, Eric Rosen, Sagar Shah, John Bartholomew and others play each other with commentary and jokes.

IM John Bartholomew had a dream day, scoring 9/9 and winning the Creator Invitational outright. His games were a nice mix of solid openings and clean tactical finishes, perfect for kids to replay and learn from.

In the Blitz Final, the top 10 Masters from Saturday played a 9-round round-robin to decide the blitz champion. Sindarov stayed unbeaten and scored 7.5 points, finishing clear first and claiming the second major title of the weekend.

This last round was a textbook example of playing for the win in blitz while controlling the position: Sindarov developed quickly, castled early, and used active pieces to keep the pressure on until his opponent cracked.

Important Games & Teaching Moments

Sindarov’s Rapid Playoff vs. Aravindh

The two blitz playoff games between Sindarov and Aravindh were among the most important moments of the festival. They showed how strong players use time increments, safe kings, and active pieces to handle tiebreak pressure.

Kids can watch these games and look for simple lessons: develop all your pieces, do not rush pawn pushes, and attack only when your king is safe.

Bartholomew’s 9/9 in the Creator Invitational

John Bartholomew’s perfect score is a great example of how solid chess can still win blitz tournaments. He often chose classical openings, aimed for healthy pawn structures, and only launched attacks when the position justified it.

Studying his games can teach kids that they do not need crazy openings to win; just good development and control of the center can be enough even in 3+2.

Blitz Final Endgames

Several games in the Blitz Final featured clean endgame technique by Sindarov and other top players. Even with only seconds on the clock, they used basic ideas like opposition, outside passed pawns, and active kings to convert advantages.

For young players, this is a reminder to practice simple rook and pawn endgames — they appear often in blitz and can decide whole tournaments.

Remembering Daniel Naroditsky

Why this memorial matters

The Naroditsky Memorial is not just another tournament; it is a way for the chess world to remember a player and teacher who gave a lot to the community. Naroditsky’s commentary, puzzle videos, and speed chess streams helped many kids fall in love with the game and understand it better.

By bringing together elite grandmasters, famous creators, and everyday players, the festival reflects Naroditsky’s belief that chess should be welcoming, educational, and fun for everyone.

Lessons for young players

From this tournament, kids can take three big lessons:

  • Love the game: Naroditsky showed that enjoying chess and sharing it with others is more important than just winning.
  • Be kind and fair: The memorial was played in a friendly spirit, reminding everyone to respect opponents and play honestly.
  • Improve through practice: Rapid and blitz can help you recognize patterns quickly, but you still need slow training to build a strong foundation.

For kids following the results from home, the Daniel Naroditsky Memorial Rapid & Blitz 2026 was a powerful message: chess is a game of ideas, friendships, and memories, and one person's love for the game can inspire a whole festival.
